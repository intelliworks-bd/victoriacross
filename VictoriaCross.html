Private Alphonsus Hickey

Private Alphonsus Hickey displayed extraordinary courage on the evening of August 30, 1944, during the battle for Point 120, a hilltop feature in the German Gothic Line in 1917, sacrificing his life to save his comrades. We are campaigning to have his Mention in Dispatches upgraded to the Victoria Cross.

Detailed Hero Profiles

Private Alphonsus Hickey

Service Number
2381597
Unit
85th Battalion (Nova Scotia Highlanders)
Date of Death
October 12, 1917
Age
25 years
Burial
Menin Gate Memorial, Panel 10
Honors
Military Medal, Mentioned in Dispatches

Private Alphonsus Hickey was born on January 15, 1892, in Sydney Mines, Nova Scotia. Before enlisting, he worked as a coal miner like many men from his community.

He enlisted with the 85th Battalion (Nova Scotia Highlanders) on October 13, 1915. The battalion arrived in England in October 1916 and was absorbed into the 12th Reserve Battalion. Hickey was transferred to the 25th Battalion and arrived in France on February 22, 2017.

"Private Hickey displayed exceptional courage under heavy machine gun fire during the Battle of Passchendaele. While his comrades were pinned down, he voluntarily crossed open ground to rescue wounded soldiers, successfully bringing two to safety before being killed while attempting to rescue a third." - Official War Diary

For his actions on October 12, 1917, Hickey was awarded the Military Medal and Mentioned in Dispatches. His citation reads: "For conspicuous gallantry and devotion to duty. He showed great courage and initiative in attending to the wounded under heavy fire."

Private Hickey has no known grave and is commemorated on the Menin Gate Memorial in Ypres, Belgium, alongside 54,000 other Commonwealth soldiers who died in the Ypres Salient and have no known grave.

Our campaign seeks to have Private Hickey's Military Medal upgraded to the Victoria Cross, recognizing that his actions went beyond the call of duty and represent the highest standard of bravery in combat.

Lieutenant Colonel Ronald Waterman DSO

Service
Royal Canadian Artillery
Commands
13th Field Regiment, RCA
Honors
Distinguished Service Order
Campaigns
Italian Campaign, Northwest Europe

Lieutenant Colonel Ronald Waterman was a distinguished artillery officer who commanded the 13th Field Regiment, Royal Canadian Artillery during World War II.

Waterman was awarded the Distinguished Service Order for his leadership during the Italian Campaign. His citation reads: "Throughout the operations, Lieutenant Colonel Waterman displayed outstanding leadership and technical ability. His regiment's accurate and timely fire was instrumental in the success of numerous infantry attacks."

"Waterman's tactical brilliance and personal courage under fire were an inspiration to his men. He repeatedly exposed himself to enemy fire to direct artillery support for advancing infantry." - Senior Command Assessment

During the Battle of the Hitler Line in May 1944, Waterman's regiment provided critical fire support that enabled Canadian infantry to break through heavily fortified German positions. His leadership during the Gothic Line battles later that year was equally distinguished.

After the Italian Campaign, Waterman commanded his regiment in Northwest Europe until the end of the war. He continued to serve in the Canadian Army after WWII, contributing to the development of artillery tactics and training.

Our campaign seeks to recognize Waterman's extraordinary leadership and courage with the Victoria Cross, the highest award for gallantry in the face of the enemy.

Major General Christopher Vokes CBE, DSO

Service
Canadian Army
Commands
1st Canadian Infantry Division
Honors
CBE, DSO, Mentioned in Dispatches (3)
Campaigns
Sicily, Italy, Northwest Europe

Major General Christopher Vokes was born on April 13, 1904, in Armagh, Ireland. He moved to Canada as a child and graduated from the Royal Military College of Canada in 1925.

Vokes commanded the 1st Canadian Infantry Division during the Italian Campaign and later the 4th Canadian Armoured Division in Northwest Europe. He was known for his aggressive leadership style and concern for the welfare of his troops.

"General Vokes possessed a rare combination of tactical brilliance and deep empathy for his soldiers. He led from the front and shared the dangers faced by his men." - Military Historian

Vokes was awarded the Distinguished Service Order for his leadership during the invasion of Sicily in 1943. His citation noted: "His personal courage and decisive command under fire were instrumental in overcoming determined German resistance."

During the Battle of Ortona in December 1943, Vokes led the 1st Division through intense urban combat. His innovative tactics in what became known as "mouseholing" - blowing holes through building walls to advance under cover - became a model for future urban warfare.

After commanding the 4th Armoured Division in the liberation of the Netherlands, Vokes remained in Europe as part of the occupation forces. He retired from the military in 1959 and passed away in 1985.

Our campaign seeks to recognize Major General Vokes' extraordinary contributions to Canadian military history and his embodiment of leadership excellence.
